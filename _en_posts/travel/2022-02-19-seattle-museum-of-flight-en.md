I visited the Museum of Flight in Seattle.

To put it simply — as the world's largest aviation museum, if you have even a passing interest in aircraft, this place is absolutely worth your time.

Founded by Boeing, the museum sits alongside King County Airport with Boeing facilities visible in the background.



---



## Entrance



![Museum of Flight Entrance](/assets/images/airline-review/museum-of-flight/IMG_3058.webp)



Flags from countries around the world line the entrance, and the glass doors read "WELCOME TO ALL." You pay admission at the welcome hall and get a wristband before heading in.



---



## Great Gallery — Aircraft Everywhere



![Great Gallery](/assets/images/airline-review/museum-of-flight/IMG_3060.webp)



Walk through the lobby and this is what greets you. Dozens of aircraft suspended from the ceiling framework, including biplanes and historic military jets.

## Aircraft Interiors



![Cabin Interior](/assets/images/airline-review/museum-of-flight/IMG_3096.webp)



An early Boeing 737 has been cut open and put on display, letting you walk through the cabin and peek inside the cockpit.



![Vintage Cockpit](/assets/images/airline-review/museum-of-flight/IMG_3098.webp)



An overwhelming sea of gauges. A completely different world compared to today's glass cockpits.

## Space Gallery



![Reentry Capsule Heat Shield](/assets/images/airline-review/museum-of-flight/IMG_3132.webp)



An actual spacecraft reentry capsule with its heat shield still showing scorch marks from atmospheric reentry. Incredible to see up close.



![Lunar Rover](/assets/images/airline-review/museum-of-flight/IMG_3109.webp)



A lunar rover model alongside actual space capsules and a Mars rover replica. Seeing hardware that traveled to space before I was even born — genuinely humbling.



---



## Aviation Pavilion — The Highlight



![Aviation Pavilion Sign](/assets/images/airline-review/museum-of-flight/IMG_3139.webp)



A massive outdoor hangar housing full-size aircraft you can actually board. Cold in winter, but without question the best part of the museum.



![Aviation Pavilion Overview](/assets/images/airline-review/museum-of-flight/IMG_3286.webp)



Air Force One, WWII bombers, commercial jets — all under one roof at jaw-dropping scale.

## Boeing 747 Prototype — "City of Everett"



![City of Everett Exterior](/assets/images/airline-review/museum-of-flight/IMG_3201.webp)



The prototype of one of aviation's greatest aircraft. The registration number — N7470 — says it all. Multiple airline liveries are painted across the fuselage.



![747 Cabin Interior](/assets/images/airline-review/museum-of-flight/IMG_3178.webp)



<div class="img-row">
  <img src="/assets/images/airline-review/museum-of-flight/IMG_3224.webp" alt="747 cargo structure">
  <img src="/assets/images/airline-review/museum-of-flight/IMG_3234.webp" alt="747 interior framework">
</div>



The sole surviving 747 Classic prototype — it served as an aerial refueling tanker, a NASA research platform, and a Boeing engine testbed before being restored and displayed here in 2015.



<div class="img-row">
  <img src="/assets/images/airline-review/museum-of-flight/IMG_3240.webp" alt="Rolls-Royce engine">
  <img src="/assets/images/airline-review/museum-of-flight/IMG_3245.webp" alt="Cockpit">
</div>



The Rolls-Royce engine pod and cockpit were accessible too.



---

That wraps up my Museum of Flight visit. Only in America. There's far more to explore than I've covered here — highly recommended for anyone with even a passing interest in aviation.
