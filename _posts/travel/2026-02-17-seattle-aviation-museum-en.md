I visited the Museum of Flight in Seattle.

To cut straight to it: if you have even a passing interest in aviation, this place is absolutely worth your time. It's the largest aviation museum in the world, and it lives up to that title.

The museum was founded by Boeing, whose campus and King County Airport are visible from the surrounding area.

![Museum Exterior](/assets/images/seattle_boeing/boeing1.webp)

Inside, the collection is staggering — real aircraft, spacecraft capsules, engines, components, and an almost overwhelming number of exhibits. I spent four hours moving at a decent pace and barely made it through everything.

## Aviation Pavilion

The Aviation Pavilion houses full-scale aircraft you can actually board and walk through — a step beyond just viewing them behind barriers.

I had originally planned to also visit the Boeing factory tour in Everett, but the tours were cancelled due to COVID, so this was the next best thing. The museum is a fair distance from downtown Seattle; I departed from a hotel near Tacoma Airport the previous night.

## Entering the Museum

At the welcome hall, you pay admission, get a wristband, and head in. As you'd expect from a museum of this nature, families with children made up the largest share of visitors.

![Inside the Main Hall](/assets/images/seattle_boeing/boeing2.webp)

Stepping through the lobby, you're immediately surrounded by dozens of aircraft — some suspended from the ceiling, others displayed on the floor. My knowledge of aircraft types was fairly limited at the time, but the sheer spectacle was enough to keep me transfixed.

Looking back through my photos now, I realize one of them was a UH-1H — a helicopter I actually crossed paths with during my military service, seeing them retired on the base. Already old acquaintances, apparently. There's also an early Boeing 737 sliced open to reveal the interior, including the cockpit, which visitors can examine up close.

## Space Gallery

The space exhibits were equally impressive — a genuine NASA capsule that had actually been to space, a Mars rover replica, and other artifacts that are rarely seen up close. Standing in front of hardware that traveled beyond Earth's atmosphere decades before I was born was a genuinely humbling experience.

![Space Gallery](/assets/images/seattle_boeing/boeing3.webp)

## The Aviation Pavilion — The Real Highlight

A vast outdoor hangar housing actual aircraft that visitors can board. It gets cold in winter ❄️, but this was the whole reason I came, and it didn't disappoint.

Inside: a Boeing 747 prototype, a Boeing 787, a Concorde, a vintage Air Force One, and other rare aircraft — not just visible, but boardable.

### Concorde

There aren't many Concordes left in existence, let alone ones you can walk through. I didn't hesitate for a second. Cockpit, first class, economy — I saw all of it. Given its legendary fuel inefficiency, the cabin is exactly what you'd expect: extraordinarily cramped. Traveling at twice the speed of sound while packed into a space like that would have required serious commitment.

![Concorde Interior](/assets/images/seattle_boeing/boeing4.webp)

### Boeing 747 Prototype (N7470)

The original prototype of the iconic Boeing 747. Even the registration number is essentially the aircraft's name. The only surviving prototype of the 747 Classic, it served as an aerial refueling tanker, was later used by NASA for research, then repurposed as a Boeing engine testbed before being retired, left in storage, and eventually fully repainted and put on display here in 2015.

Standing next to it in person, the scale is genuinely imposing.

![Boeing 747 Prototype](/assets/images/seattle_boeing/boeing5.webp)

---

That wraps up my visit to the Museum of Flight. "Only in America" was a phrase that came to mind more than once. Beyond what I've described, there are countless hands-on experiences and exhibits throughout the museum. For anyone who loves aviation, this place is an easy recommendation — it was an easy highlight of the whole trip.
