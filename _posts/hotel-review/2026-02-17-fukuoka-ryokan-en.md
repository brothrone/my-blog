I took a direct bus from Fukuoka Airport and arrived at Yufuin Station three hours later.

![Yufuin Station](/assets/images/fuk_1/fuk_a_20.webp)

It had been a while since my last visit to Yufuin. My previous trip had been so wonderful that returning here felt like coming home.

This was also my first time back in Japan since COVID restrictions had eased — stepping off the bus still felt slightly surreal, even as I was overcome with happiness. Just a week prior I had been in the army...

## Yufuin Seven-Eleven

![Seven-Eleven](/assets/images/fuk_1/fuk_a_19.webp)

The Seven-Eleven I always stop at in Yufuin. From the station entrance, head right and walk just a short distance — the coffee here is genuinely excellent ☕️

![Convenience Store](/assets/images/fuk_1/fuk_a_17.webp)

![Paying at the Store](/assets/images/fuk_1/fuk_a_18.webp)

Since the ryokan is tucked deep into the mountains, stocking up at the convenience store beforehand is essential. The kaiseki served at the ryokan is outstanding, of course, but convenience store snacks are a non-negotiable addition.

![Taxi](/assets/images/fuk_1/fuk_a_6.webp)

Into the deep mountain valleys by taxi...

![Snowy Mountain](/assets/images/fuk_1/fuk_a_2.webp)

It had snowed not long before our arrival, and the mountains were blanketed in white.

## Oyado Yufunosho Ryokan

![Ryokan Exterior](/assets/images/fuk_1/fuk_a_4.webp)

I had visited this ryokan once before with my family and loved it so much that I returned. Nearly five years had passed, yet it was exactly as I remembered.

![Ryokan in Snow](/assets/images/fuk_1/fuk_a_5.webp)

Snow had fallen on our previous visit too, and this time we arrived to find it already piled up around the property.

![Ryokan Interior](/assets/images/fuk_1/fuk_a_1.webp)

![Ryokan Interior 2](/assets/images/fuk_1/fuk_a_3.webp)

The interior of the ryokan. With six people in our group, we booked two rooms. By a stroke of luck, one of our rooms turned out to be the exact same room we'd stayed in years before — a tatami room with its own private open-air bath.

![Behind the Ryokan](/assets/images/fuk_1/fuk_a_16.webp)

The view from the back door of the ryokan — pure wilderness.

![Second Room](/assets/images/fuk_1/fuk_a_8.webp)

The second room was smaller and didn't have a private outdoor bath, but was perfectly comfortable for two people.

![Welcome Sweets](/assets/images/fuk_1/fuk_a_15.webp)

The welcome treat was the same dango (rice dumplings) as last time — incredibly good. The tea in the thermos beside it was equally delightful.

![Private Onsen](/assets/images/fuk_1/fuk_a_9.webp)

We immediately made use of the private open-air hot spring in our room.

![Onsen View](/assets/images/fuk_1/fuk_a_7.webp)

The view from the in-room onsen. Snow falling outside made the atmosphere even more picturesque.

## Ryokan Kaiseki Dinner

One of the highlights of any ryokan stay in Japan is the kaiseki — a traditional multi-course seasonal meal. The kaiseki here was so memorable on my first visit that it was the primary reason I came back.

![Kaiseki Table Setting](/assets/images/fuk_1/fuk_a_14.webp)

A staff member led us to a room where the table was already beautifully set. The spread included traditional Japanese dishes, chicken, beef, and horse meat.

Japanese kaiseki lets the natural flavor of each ingredient speak for itself, without heavy seasoning or spices — and it's all the richer for it.

![Kaiseki 2](/assets/images/fuk_1/fuk_a_10.webp)

![Kaiseki 3](/assets/images/fuk_1/fuk_a_12.webp)

The courses kept coming, though compared to our pre-COVID visit, the portions were slightly smaller and a few dishes had been removed from the lineup — a minor disappointment.

![Latte by the Hearth](/assets/images/fuk_1/fuk_a_13.webp)

Just outside the dining room is a central open hearth — ordering a latte and sipping it by the fire after dinner is pure bliss. A warm drink beside a glowing hearth on a cold winter night ☕️

![Communal Onsen](/assets/images/fuk_1/fuk_a_11.webp)

After dinner, we headed to the shared hot spring bath. Like most ryokan communal baths, it operates on an honor system with a sign you hang to indicate it's occupied.

Outside it was below freezing, but inside the onsen, the cold completely disappeared — the sensation of being submerged in hot water while the air around you is icy is one of those genuinely irreplaceable pleasures.

We only stayed one night, but this ryokan left such an impression that another return visit feels almost inevitable.
